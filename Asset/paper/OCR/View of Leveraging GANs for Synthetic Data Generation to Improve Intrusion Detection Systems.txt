Abstract: This research presents a hybrid intrusion detection approach that integrates Generative Ad-
versarial Networks (GANs) for synthetic data generation with Random Forest (RF) as the primary
classifier. The study aims to improve detection performance in cybersecurity applications by enhancing
dataset diversity and addressing challenges in traditional models, particularly in detecting minority at-
tack classes often underrepresented in real-world datasets. The proposed method employs GANs to
generate synthetic attack samples that mimic real-world intrusions, which are then combined with real
data from the UNSW-NB15 dataset to create a more balanced training set. By leveraging synthetic data
augmentation, our approach mitigates issues related to class imbalance and enhances the generalization
capability of the classifier. Extensive experiments demonstrate that RF trained on the combined dataset
of real and synthetic data achieves superior detection performance compared to models trained exclu-
sively on real data. Specifically, RF trained solely on the original dataset achieves an accuracy of 97.58%,
whereas integrating GAN-generated synthetic data improves accuracy to 98.27%. The proposed meth-
odology is further evaluated through comparative analysis against alternative classifiers, including Sup-
port Vector Machine (SVM), XGBoost, Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU), and related studies in the field.
Our findings indicate that GAN-augmented training significantly enhances detection rates, particularly
for rare attack types, while maintaining computational efficiency. Furthermore, RF outperforms other
classifiers, including deep learning models, demonstrating its effectiveness as a lightweight yet robust
classification method. Integrating GANs with RF offers a scalable and adaptable framework for intru-

sion detection, ensuring improved resilience against evolving cyber threats.

Keywords: Adversarial Learning; Anomaly Detection; Cybersecurity; Cyber Threat Detection;
Intrusion Detection System; Random Forest; Synthetic Data Generation.

1. Introduction

Cybersecurity has become a critical concern in today's interconnected world, where tech-
nological advancements are rapidly evolving. As the digital landscape expands, so does the
potential for cyber threats, making it imperative to advance cybersecurity research[1]. This
article aims to explore the primary objectives and necessities for conducting cybersecurity
research, referencing relevant publications highlighting this field's significance and depth.

The advent of 5G technology has transformed access environments, establishing dis-
tributed networks for diverse data from sensors, computers, and JoT. Despite expanded pro-
cessing capacities, the increased access points heighten vulnerability to cyber threats[2], [3].
The cybersecurity challenge of effectively detecting network threats drives extensive research
in network intrusion detection systems (NIDSs). Initially, research focused on traditional
models like decision trees (DTs) [4] and support vector machines (SVMs) [5], evolving to
advanced deep learning approaches CNNs, LSTMs, and autoencoders[6], [7]. Despite

The prevailing issue of data imbalance in network intrusion detection systems (NIDSs)

One of the core objectives of cybersecurity research is to identify, analyze, and develop
methods to detect and prevent emerging cyber threats[8]. One research work delves into ma-

incident response and recovery mechanisms to minimize the impact of cyber-attacks[11], [12].
Also, Bejtlich et al. [13] offer a comprehensive guide to incident response procedures.

In the past, researchers have investigated a wide range of approaches to detect and mit-
igate cyberattacks effectively. These efforts have included the use of traditional machine learn-
ing[14]—[19], techniques as well as more advanced methodologies, such as deep learning [20]—

ods have successfully identified vulnerabilities, detected threats, and prevented malicious ac-

Recently, cybersecurity research has increasingly leveraged Large Language Models
(LLMs) to enhance the effectiveness of threat detection solutions [25], [26]. LLMs have been
shown to process and understand complex patterns in textual data, proving valuable in ana-
lyzing code, identifying vulnerabilities, and predicting cyber threats. Prior studies have fine-
tuned pre-trained LLMs using supervised learning on task-specific labeled datasets, such as
those from deepset, available via Hugging Face[27]. This methodology aligns with previous
research, including Rahman et al. [25], which utilized multi-lingual BERT for dataset embed-
ding and achieved a high accuracy of 96.55% using logistic regression. In that study, BERT
embeddings significantly improved model performance by capturing nuanced patterns in the
dataset.

In previous research[28], BERT has been applied to generate embeddings for code pat-
terns within cybersecurity datasets. These embeddings were then used as inputs for various
machine learning models to analyze their effectiveness in detecting cybersecurity vulnerabili-
ties. Furthermore, supervised fine- -tuning was employed on a pre-trained LLM, adapting it to
specific tasks using labeled data from deepset on Hugging Face. This approach has enhanced
the model's ability to understand and predict complex relationships within the data, pushing
the boundaries of LLM-based cybersecurity solutions.

Generative Adversarial Networks (GANS) i is one of the best approaches, which can sig-
nificantly contribute to cybersecurity research by enhancing various aspects of threat detec-
tion, response, and defense mechanisms [29]. GANs consist of two competing neural net-
works: a generator, which produces synthetic data, and a discriminator, which evaluates the
authenticity of the generated samples[30]. These models have been successfully applied to
cybersecurity research, such as generating synthetic malware samples for better detection,
simulating normal network behavior for anomaly detection, and conducting adversarial test-
ing to identify weaknesses in IDS. GANs also facilitate malware transformation into different
representations to improve classification accuracy. Additionally, they can create adversarial
attacks to test the robustness of security systems and generate cybersecurity data visualizations
that aid in understanding attack patterns. However, while GANs offer substantial benefits in
cybersecurity, they can also be leveraged by malicious actors to generate sophisticated cyber
threats, emphasizing the need for continuous evolution in defensive strategies [31].

classifier due to its efficiency, interpretability, and ability to handle high-dimensional data with
less risk of overfitting. RF is an ensemble learning method that constructs multiple decision

2. Literature Review

2.1. Dataset

The University of New South Wales Network-Based 15 (UNSW-NB15) dataset com-
ptises 49 features and 2,540,044 instances, making it a valuable resource for network security
research. Each instance represents a snapshot of network activity over a specific time period,
capturing both normal and malicious traffic. The dataset includes various types of attacks,
such as denial-of-service (DoS), distributed denial-of-service (DDoS), probing, and malware,
ensuring a comprehensive representation of cyber threats. Simulating realistic network envi-
ronments provides an essential benchmark for evaluating the performance of intrusion de-
tection systems (IDSs) performance in practical settings [33].

A notable characteristic of the UNSW-NB15 dataset is its extensive annotation, where
each network flow is labeled according to its traffic type, distinguishing between normal and
malicious activities. This structured labeling facilitates the training and validation of machine
learning models, allowing for effective cybersecurity research. Furthermore, given the sub-
stantial network traffic volume, the dataset is a robust benchmark for assessing detection
mechanisms, particularly in high-traffic scenarios where scalability and efficiency are crucial.

2.2. Loss Function of GANs

Network intrusion detection systems (NIDS) currently employ various machine learning
algorithms that achieve high performance. However, they often face challenges in classifying
rare attack classes in large-scale traffic scenarios, and their practical effectiveness against di-
verse cyber threats remains uncertain[34]. Previous studies have compared the precision and
accuracy of different machine learning algorithms on the NSL-KDD dataset, varying in size
(full, half, and quarter)[35]. An IoT-tailored IDS research framework has been proposed to
address IoT-related challenges, considering the constraints of applying existing techniques to
security threats and detection methods [36]. Traditional NIDS research, essential for security
in conventional and IoT networks, has often focused on small datasets and classical machine
learning approaches.

Recent approaches have leveraged larger datasets incorporating recent cyber threats and
deep learning methodologies to overcome these limitations and enhance classification capa-
bilities. Using high-volume traffic datasets, deep learning-based NIDS has demonstrated ro-
bust performance against various attack types in real-world scenarios, emphasizing practical
applicability. This paper contributes to NIDS research by utilizing extensive datasets and
state-of-the-art deep learning techniques, effectively bridging the gap between theoretical ad-
vancements and real-world implementation. The objective is to significantly enhance the clas-
sification capabilities of NIDS, particularly in large-scale network traffic and evolving cyber
threat landscapes.

GANs, introduced by Goodfellow et al. [30], have gained substantial recognition due to
their broad applicability in various fields, including cybersecurity. As illustrated in Figure 2,
the GAN framework consists of two primary components: a generator network (G) and a
discriminator network (D). The generator (G) acts as a data synthesizer, receiving a random
noise vector (Z) as input, which follows a Gaussian or uniform distribution. Through a learned
mapping function, the generator transforms Z into synthetic samples, denoted as G(zZ),
which approximate the distribution of real data[37].

3. Proposed Method

This research proposes a hybrid methodology to improve intrusion detection perfor-
mance by addressing the challenges posed by imbalanced cybersecurity datasets. The ap-
proach integrates Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) for synthetic data generation
with Random Forest (RF) as the primary classification model. GANs are employed to create
synthetic attack samples, which are then combined with real data to improve class balance.
This technique reduces data loss and overfitting risks, ensuring better generalization across
different attack types.

Figure 2, introduced in Section 2.2, presents the general architecture of the GAN model,
while Figure 3 illustrates the overall workflow of the proposed hybrid training process. In this
approach, GAN-generated synthetic data is integrated into the RF training pipeline, directly
comparing RF trained on real data and RF trained with augmented data to measure the impact
of GAN-based oversampling.

The proposed GAN architecture consists of a generator and a discriminator:

e The generator comprises fully connected layers with LeakyReLU activation, generating
synthetic attack samples that mimic real-world scenarios.

e The discriminator classifies real versus synthetic data using binary cross-entropy loss,
ensuring that only high-quality synthetic samples contribute to dataset augmentation.

e = Both networks are trained using the Adam optimizer (learning rate = 0.0002, 61=0.5)
for stable convergence. The GAN model undergoes 10,000 epochs of training with a
batch size of 64, ensuring the generation of reliable synthetic samples.

e The final dataset is normalized using min-max scaling to balance feature distributions.
The dataset is then split into 70% training and 30% testing subsets to optimize the clas-
sification process.

Random Forest (RF) is selected as the primary classifier to assess the impact of synthetic
data augmentation. The RF model is configured with 100 estimators, providing a robust and
efficient baseline for evaluating the effectiveness of GAN-augmented data. RF was chosen
due to its ability to handle high-dimensional data, its resilience against class imbalance, and its
efficiency compared to deep learning methods.

Through extensive analysis of cybersecurity datasets, this approach ensures a more bal-
anced data distribution by integrating GAN-generated synthetic samples into the training
pipeline. The adversarial learning framework in GANs enhances data diversity, producing
more realistic network traffic patterns. This augmentation strategy significantly improves da-
taset quality, optimizing feature representations before classification.

Integrating GANs with RF introduces a scalable and practical solution for handling im-
balanced datasets, particularly in intrusion detection systems. By ensuring that minority attack
classes receive adequate representation, the proposed approach enhances dataset prepro-
cessing strategies for cybersecurity applications.

4. Results and Discussion

The proposed model was implemented using Python with the pandas, TensorFlow, and
Keras libraries on the Google Colab platform. The training process involved multiple GAN
experiments, varying the number of epochs (100, 1000, and 10000) to analyze how the gen-
erator and discriminator loss evolved over time. For the initial 100 and 1000 epochs, training
was conducted with a learning rate of 0.0004 and 61=0.5, whereas in the final proposed model
at 10,000 epochs, a more stable learning rate of 0.0002 was selected to improve convergence.
These adjustments aimed to balance generator improvement while preventing mode collapse
in longer training phases. Figure 4 presents these experiments, showcasing the learning be-
havior of GANs under different conditions.

Based on Figure 4, for the initial 100 epochs, the Generator and Discriminator show
distinct learning patterns. The generator's loss starts at a high value and gradually decreases
as training progresses. The minimum recorded loss for the Generator is 0.33, while the max-
imum reaches 14.45, indicating that the model is actively adjusting to synthesize realistic data.
Meanwhile, the discriminator's loss stabilizes within the range of 0.12 to 2.6, suggesting that
it effectively distinguishes between real and synthetic data.

The results further demonstrate the impact of synthetic data augmentation in enhancing
classifier performance. When trained on the original UNSW-NB15 dataset, RF achieved an
accuracy of 97.58%. However, upon integrating GAN-generated synthetic samples, the accu-
racy increased to 98.27%, reflecting the role of synthetic data in improving feature diversity
and classifier robustness. A similar trend is observed for XGBoost, where model accuracy
improved from 96.04% to 96.78% after incorporating synthetic data.

From a deep learning perspective, GRU-based models exhibited competitive perfor-
mance, achieving 95.53% accuracy on the UNSW-NB15 dataset. However, when combined
with GAN-generated data, the accuracy increased to 96.21%, reinforcing the role of synthetic
augmentation in improving model generalization. The SVM+GAN model also improved
over standard SVM (94.11% vs. 92.28%), though it remained less competitive than RF-based
approaches.

These results suggest that integrating GAN-generated synthetic data improves the gen-
eralization ability of classifiers, particularly for complex datasets such as UNSW-NB15, which
contains a diverse range of attack patterns. Moreover, the UNSW-NB15 dataset exhibited
higher stability when generating synthetic data. In summary, the findings underscore the effi-
cacy of the proposed RF+GAN approach in intrusion detection, demonstrating superior per-
formance over existing state-of-the-art methods. The integration of synthetic data not only
mitigates dataset imbalance but also enhances model robustness, ultimately leading to more
reliable cyberattack detection. These results further emphasize the importance of adversarial
learning in cybersecurity applications, where traditional classifiers may struggle with un-
derrepresented attack patterns.

5. Conclusions

The research presented in this study focuses on advancing deep learning technologies
for network intrusion detection, particularly addressing imbalanced data challenges. The pro-
posed approach leverages RF for classifiers and GANs to generate synthetic data, aiming to
enhance the detection performance of intrusion detection systems. The comparative analysis,
as depicted in Table II, highlights the efficacy of the proposed model against existing state-
of-the-art approaches. Most importantly, our RF model achieved an accuracy of 97.58% when
trained on the existing UNSW-NB15 dataset. However, the true strength of our methodology
emerges when combining synthetic and real datasets, resulting in a substantial accuracy im-
provement to 98.27%. This underscores the significance of addressing data imbalance
through the integration of synthetic data, showcasing the model's adaptability and improved
performance. In contrast to the existing models, our proposed approach consistently outper-
forms them, surpassing advanced algorithms. The robustness of the proposed model is par-
ticularly evident in handling the complex UNSW-NB15 dataset, where traditional machine-
learning models exhibit limitations. In summary, the research demonstrates the effectiveness
of the proposed RF model with a combined dataset in mitigating imbalanced data issues,
thereby contributing significantly to the advancement of intrusion detection systems. The
results affirm the potential of incorporating synthetic data in training deep learning models,
presenting a promising avenue for future research in the realm of network security.
